Smart Band Controller

A wrist-worn motion controller for driving a wall-projected thinking surface by gesture — so a visual memory palace isn’t tied to a keyboard, and you can work the whole wall from across the room.

Role  Design & build· Stack  MediaPipe, IR camera, ESP32, BNO055 IMU, BLE· ● Building Mark II

The idea

I think associatively and spatially — across domains, by laying things next to each other. A large projected canvas is the right surface for that: an interactive whiteboard, a memory palace you can actually walk up to. But keyboard and mouse tether you to the desk and collapse that space back into a screen. The goal here is to stand back and manipulate the projected wall directly, in the air — closer to how you’d move things on a physical board than drive an application.

To be clear about scope: this isn’t about generating content — voice and AI agents already handle that. It’s the opposite problem — an external surface for thinking visually, navigated by hand.

Approach

Two input paths feed the same control layer. A camera tracks hand landmarks with MediaPipe; an IR night-vision camera keeps that working in a darkened projection room. And a wrist-worn band — an ESP32 with a BNO055 IMU — reads orientation and motion directly, so control doesn’t depend on staying in frame and gains precise tilt and rotation.

  • MediaPipe hand tracking over an IR camera for low-light projection rooms.
  • Wrist band: ESP32 + BNO055 IMU streaming orientation over Bluetooth (Mark II, in progress).
  • A deterministic gesture-to-action layer: pan, zoom, select, and move nodes on the projected canvas.

Where it’s at

The camera-and-projection rig works today; the Mark II wristband — the ESP32 + IMU controller that makes it precise and untethered — is the piece I’m building now. The end state is a wall you drive with your hands: pull a cluster of ideas closer, push another away, arrange a thought like it’s a physical object.

What it demonstrates

Embedded firmware, computer vision, sensor fusion, and interaction design in one build — evidence that I work where software meets physical hardware, not just at the API. It’s the most personal thing here: a tool built to fit how my own mind works.
